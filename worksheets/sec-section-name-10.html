    Worksheet Math 330 Delta Functions for Real
    

    A key construction in the theory of PDEs is the \(\delta\)-function. Indeed, we used it to solve the Cauchy problem for the heat equation and will use it in proving the Fourier Inversion Theorem. The key property is that
    \begin{equation*} (f\ast \delta)(x) = f(x), \end{equation*}
    where the convolution is defined as
    \begin{equation*} (f\ast g)(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x-y)g(y)\,dy. \end{equation*}
    Note: We will need some assumptions about \(f\) and \(g\) to ensure the convolution converges (see theorem below). In order to really understand this fundamental property, we’re just going to study the \(\delta\)-function for real.
    🔗

    Definition 5.

    A \(\delta\)-net for a class of functions (e.g bounded and continuous on \(\mathbb{R}\)) is a parametrized family of functions \(\{g_{\alpha}\}\) with \(\alpha >0\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{\alpha\to 0^+} (f\ast g_{\alpha})(x) = f(x) \end{equation*}
    whenever \(f\) is in the indicated family.
    🔗
    🔗
    The idea here is that \(\delta(x) = \lim_{\alpha\to 0} g_{\alpha}(x)\) whenever \(g_{\alpha}\) is a \(\delta\)-net. Our goal now is to show how we can build \(\delta\)-nets. To this end, suppose \(g\) is a non-negative function such that
    \begin{equation*} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x)\,dx = 1. \end{equation*}
    From this, we consider the family given by \(g_{\alpha}(x) = \dfrac{1}{\alpha} g\left(\dfrac{x}{\alpha}\right)\text{,}\) where \(\alpha>0\text{.}\)
    🔗

    1.

    Show that \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g_{\alpha}(x)\,dx = 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    We will now prove the following theorem in a series of exercises:
    🔗

    Theorem 6.

    Suppose \(f\) is a continuous function on \(\mathbb{R}\) such that \(|f(x)|\leq M\) for all \(x\in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) With \(g_{\alpha}\) as above,
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{\alpha\to 0^+} (f\ast g_{\alpha})(x) = f(x). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    We prove this by showing that for any \(\epsilon >0\) and \(x\in\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) there exists an \(A>0\) such that whenever \(\alpha < A\text{,}\) \(|f(x) - (f\ast g_{\alpha})(x)| <\epsilon\text{.}\)
    🔗

    2.

    Why does showing that prove the theorem?
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    Show that
    \begin{align*} |f(x) - (f\ast g_{\alpha})(x)| = \amp \left| \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} (f(x) - f(x-y))g_{\alpha}(y)\, dy\right|\\ \leq \amp \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |f(x) - f(x-y)|g_{\alpha}(y)\, dy. \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗

    4.

    Using the continuity of \(f\text{,}\) show that there exists a \(\delta > 0\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \int_{-\delta}^{\delta} |f(x) - f(x-y)| g_{\alpha}(y)\,dy < \frac{\epsilon}{2}, \end{equation*}
    when \(|y|<\delta\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Using the constant \(\delta\) from the previous exercise, we now have
    \begin{align*} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |f(x) - f(x-y)| g_{\alpha}(y)\,dy= \amp \int_{-\infty}^{-\delta} |f(x) - f(x-y)| g_{\alpha}(y)\,dy + \\ \amp \int_{-\delta}^{\delta} |f(x) - f(x-y)| g_{\alpha}(y)\,dy +\\ \amp \int_{\delta}^{\infty} |f(x) - f(x-y)| g_{\alpha}(y)\,dy\\ < \amp \int_{-\infty}^{-\delta} |f(x) - f(x-y)| g_{\alpha}(y)\,dy + \frac{\epsilon}{2} + \\ \amp \int_{\delta}^{\infty} |f(x) - f(x-y)| g_{\alpha}(y)\,dy \end{align*}
    Now we need to make the two "tail" integrals small by choosing \(\alpha\) correctly.
    🔗

    5.

    Argue that
    \begin{align*} \int_{\delta}^{\infty} |f(x) - f(x-y)| g_{\alpha}(y)\, dy \leq \amp \frac{2M}{\alpha} \int_{\delta}^{\infty} g\left(\frac{y}{\alpha}\right)\,dy\\ = \amp 2M \int_{\delta/\alpha}^{\infty} g(u)\,du \end{align*}
    and
    \begin{align*} \int_{-\infty}^{-\delta} |f(x) - f(x-y)| g_{\alpha}(y)\, dy \leq \amp \frac{2M}{\alpha} \int_{-\infty}^{-\delta} g\left(\frac{y}{\alpha}\right)\,dy\\ = \amp 2M \int_{-\infty}^{-\delta/\alpha} g(u)\,du. \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Now, because \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x) \,dx = 1\text{,}\) there exists a \(b>0\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \int_{-\infty}^{-b} g(u)\,du + \int_{b}^{\infty} g(u)\,du = 1-\int_{-b}^{b} g(u)\,du < \frac{\epsilon}{4M}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    6.

    Give a brief reason that the last sentence is true, then use its conclusion to complete the proof of the theorem.
    🔗
    🔗

    7.

    Explain the use of this theorem in proving Theorem 2.7 in your textbook. In other words, what’s the \(\delta\)-net and what is it being used for in this theorem? (Assume \(\phi\) is continuous rather than just peicewise continuous.)
