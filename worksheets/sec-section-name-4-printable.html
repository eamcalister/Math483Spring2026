    Worksheet Math 483 Homework 1
    

    Objectives

    • Review uniform vs. pointwise convergence.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Prove that Riemann integrals are well behaved with respect to uniform convergence.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Our main goal this semester is to prove a theorem like Theorem 3.15 in your textbook (just a little different). To do so, we need to understand uniform convergence, and why it is nice.
    🔗

    Definition 1.

    Let \(b > a\text{.}\) We say the sequence \(\{f_n\}_{n=0}^{\infty}\) converges pointwise (written as \(f_n\to f\)) to \(f\) on \([a,b]\) if for each \(x\in [a,b]\) and \(\epsilon > 0\text{,}\) there is some \(N\in \mathbb{N}\) such that if \(n\geq N\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} |f_n(x) - f(x)| < \epsilon. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.

    Let \(b > a\text{.}\) We say the sequence \(\{f_n\}_{n=0}^{\infty}\) converges uniformy (written as \(f_n\xrightarrow[]{u}f\)) to \(f\) on \([a,b]\) if for each \(\epsilon > 0 \text{,}\) there is some \(N\in \mathbb{N}\) such that if \(n\geq N\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} |f_n(x) - f(x)| < \epsilon \end{equation*}
    for all \(x\in [a,b]\)
    🔗
    🔗

    1. The Standard Example.

    Let \(f_n(x) = x^n\) and let
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \begin{cases} 0, & x\in [0,1)\\ 1, & x=1. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Prove that \(f_n \to f\) pointwise on \([0,1]\text{,}\) but not uniformly.
    🔗
    🔗

    2. Another Example.

    Let
    \begin{equation*} f_n(x) = \begin{cases} n, & x\in (0,1/n)\\ 0, & x\in [1/n,1]\cup \{0\}. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Prove that \(f_n\to 0\) on \([0,1]\text{,}\) but not uniformly.
    🔗
    🔗
    Now we have an important theorem about uniform convergence.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.

    Let \(\{f_n\}_{n=0}^{\infty}\) be a sequence of functions that are Riemann integrable on \([a,b]\text{.}\) If \(f_n\xrightarrow[]{u}f\text{,}\) then \(f\) is Riemann integrable and
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to\infty}\int_a^b f_n(x)\,dx = \int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    Prove the theorem given above. Here are some hints:
    • Find an element of the sequence that is uniformly close to \(f\text{.}\) Use this to argue that \(f\) is bounded on \([a,b]\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Find a partition of \([a,b]\) so that the upper sum and lower sum of the function approximating \(f\) are close to one another.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Argue that this implies the upper and lower sums of \(f\) over this partition are close to one another. This will get you integrability.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Now argue the integral of \(f\) is the limit of the integral of the \(f_n\)’s.
      🔗
      🔗
    You should be making some \(\epsilon/3\) arguments here.
    🔗
    🔗

    4.

    Use one of the examples above to show uniform convergence is necessary in the previous theorem.
    🔗
